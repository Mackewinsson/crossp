Buyers ask “How long does an MVP take?” because they have a fundraising deadline, a sales window, or a board update. The honest answer: it depends on what you call an MVP — and most teams inflate the label until it means “almost the full product.”

## What an MVP is (and is not)

An MVP is the **smallest production slice** that lets a real user complete one valuable job. It is not:

- a clickable prototype with no backend,
- a full multi-role platform “because we will need it later,”
- or a feature list copied from a competitor.

If your “MVP” includes every edge case, every admin report, and every integration, you are building v2 with an MVP label.

## Timeline bands that are usually honest

Again: bands, not guarantees.

- **2–4 weeks:** very narrow slice (auth + one core flow + deploy) when scope is already clear and integrations are light.
- **4–8 weeks:** typical focused MVP with admin basics, staging, and production readiness.
- **8–12+ weeks:** payments, complex roles, AI features, or messy legacy integrations enter the picture.

Anything “done in 10 days” that claims full product quality usually skips tests, handover, or real production hardening.

## What “done” should mean

Agree this in writing before the clock starts:

- Users can complete the primary workflow end to end.
- Code lives in **your** repository; credentials and cloud are under **your** accounts.
- Staging exists; production deploy is documented and repeatable.
- Critical paths have automated tests or at least a clear QA checklist.
- You can demo weekly progress, not just receive status emails.

> A launch date without a definition of done is just a rumor with a calendar invite.

## How to compress time without destroying quality

- Cut scope before you cut quality.
- Sequence: core workflow → admin essentials → secondary integrations.
- Prefer weekly shippable increments over a big-bang reveal.
- Make decisions fast — buyer delay is often the real schedule killer.
