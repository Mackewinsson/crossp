“How much does custom software cost?” is the first question most buyers ask — and the worst one to answer with a single number. In 2026, price still depends on scope, integrations, compliance, and whether you buy a black-box project or a visible sprint partnership.

## What actually drives the price

- **Scope clarity.** Vague requirements get padded with contingency. Clear MVP scope costs less to estimate and less to build.
- **Integrations.** Connecting payments, ERPs, CRMs, or legacy systems often costs more than the UI.
- **Quality bar.** Auth, roles, audit logs, tests, CI/CD, and staging environments are not optional for real products — and they show up in the budget.
- **Who builds it.** Senior architecture up front is usually cheaper than junior speed that needs a rewrite.
- **Delivery model.** A fixed quote on evolving requirements tends to grow via change orders. Sprint-based work prices learning as you go.

## Realistic ranges (order of magnitude)

Treat these as planning bands, not quotes:

- **Landing + lead capture + simple admin:** often in the low thousands to low five figures, depending on design depth and integrations.
- **Focused MVP (auth, core workflow, admin, production deploy):** commonly five figures, sometimes low six if AI, payments, or complex data are involved.
- **Multi-role product with integrations and compliance needs:** often mid-to-high five figures and above, especially if you need ongoing iteration after launch.

Geography, seniority, and timeline pressure move these bands. Offshore “cheap” rates can become expensive if ownership, tests, and handover are missing.

## Fixed quote vs sprint pricing

A large fixed quote feels safe. It often is not — because it freezes assumptions before you have user feedback. Sprint pricing (or a small discovery + first sprint) lets you pay for working software you can reject, not for a promise locked in a PDF.

> If the only number you have is a big fixed quote and no weekly demo plan, you are buying hope.

## How to budget without getting trapped

- Fund a **first shippable slice**, not the entire roadmap.
- Require **repository + cloud under your accounts** from day one.
- Ask what is **included by default** (tests, CI, documentation, staging).
- Keep a reserve for the second and third iterations — products rarely stop at v1.
